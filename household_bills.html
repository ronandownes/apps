16.4 Household Bills

Utility bills (electricity, gas) have two components:

  • Standing charge – fixed daily amount (pay regardless of usage)
  • Unit charge – cost per kWh of electricity used
Total Cost = Standing Charge + (Units Used × Unit Rate) + VAT

Understanding the Standing Charge

The graph shows that even with zero usage, you still pay the standing charge (the y-intercept). The slope of the line is the unit rate.

16.5 Ratio and Proportion

Ratios are used to divide or share quantities.

🔗 Connection to y = mx + c

Remember from coordinate geometry: y = mx + c describes a straight line where m is the slope and c is the y-intercept.

Our electricity bill follows this pattern exactly:

Cost = 0.141 × kWh + 14.62
y = m × x + c

But here's the big discovery: when c ≠ 0 (the line doesn't pass through the origin), we cannot use direct proportion with absolute values!

⚠️ Why Absolute Values Don't Work

Situation: A streaming service charges a €10 standing fee plus €2 per unit (downloads/hours/etc.)

Cost = 2 × units + 10

UnitsCost (€)Calculation
4€182 × 4 + 10 = 18
12€342 × 12 + 10 = 34

Test for proportionality: Are these ratios equal?

34/12 = 2.83...     vs     18/4 = 4.5

2.83 ≠ 4.5 — NOT proportional!

💡 Cross-Multiplication Test (No Calculator Needed!)

To check if a/b = c/d, compute the cross products:
If a × d = b × c → Ratios are equal ✓
If a × d ≠ b × c → Ratios are not equal ✗

Our example: Is 34/12 = 18/4?

Cross multiply: 34 × 4 = 136    vs    12 × 18 = 216

136 ≠ 216 → Not proportional!

✅ The Solution: Use Relative Values

When the line doesn't pass through the origin, all is not lost! We can still find proportionality using changes (relative values) instead of absolute values.

Key insight: The change in cost is proportional to the change in units.

UnitsCost (€)Change in UnitsChange in Cost
4€18——
12€3412 − 4 = 834 − 18 = €16

Check: €16 ÷ 8 units = €2 per unit ✓

This matches our rate! The slope (m = 2) gives us the constant of proportionality for the changes.

Δy = m × Δx
Change in cost = rate × change in units
📐 Summary: When is there Direct Proportion?

Through originy = mxYes — use absolute values
Not through originy = mx + c (c ≠ 0)No — use relative values (changes)
Each share = parts/total parts × amount
💡 Method: (1) Add ratio numbers to find total parts. (2) Calculate each share as a fraction of the total.

Worked Example 16.11

A pizza has 14 slices, shared between Alan, Brian, and Ciara in ratio 2 : 2 : 3.

Solution

Total parts = 2 + 2 + 3 = 7
Alan: ²⁄₇ × 14 = 4 slices
Brian: ²⁄₇ × 14 = 4 slices
Ciara: ³⁄₇ × 14 = 6 slices

Worked Example 16.12

Jack (8) and Danny (10) share money in ratio of ages. Danny gets €60.

(a) How much in the money box? (b) How much did Jack get?

Solution

Ratio 8 : 10. Danny's 10 parts = €60 → 1 part = €6
(a) Total = 18 × €6 = €108
(b) Jack = 8 × €6 = €48

Worked Example 16.13 – Direct Proportion

Allie gets €12 for 2 hours of chores.

Solution

Rate = €12 ÷ 2 = €6/hour
(a) 3 hours = 3 × €6 = €18
(b) €36 ÷ €6 = 6 hours

Value for Money

💡 Method: Compare price per 100g (or per litre). Lower = better value.

Worked Example 16.14

Biscuits: 140g for €1.50 vs 230g for €3.00

Solution

140g pack: €1.50 ÷ 1.4 = €1.07/100g
230g pack: €3.00 ÷ 2.3 = €1.30/100g
140g pack is better value

Worked Example 16.15

Pasta: 1kg fusilli €3.19 vs 750g shells €1.69

Solution

Fusilli: €3.19 ÷ 10 = €0.32/100g
Shells: €1.69 ÷ 7.5 = €0.23/100g
750g shells are better value
